Dillard's Cosmetics Data Analysis

A Comprehensive SQL & Tableau Case Study

Abstract

Advanced technologies have transformed how accounting professionals handle data, making accounting increasingly a STEM-related field that requires strong data analytics and technology skills. This case study gives students hands-on experience using SQL and Tableau to perform real-world data analysis. It covers tasks like data extraction with SQL, data visualization using word clouds, geographic maps, and boxplots, data grouping through clustering, and communicating insights via interactive dashboards and storytelling. The goal is to help students appreciate how SQL and Tableau can be powerful tools for analyzing and visualizing large datasets, while developing a strong data analytics mindset.

Case Study

Dillard's, Inc. is a luxury department store chain headquartered in Little Rock, Arkansas, founded in 1938 by William T. Dillard. It expanded rapidly through acquisitions in the 1960s and became a public company in 1969. Today, it operates about 282 stores across 29 states, with Texas (57) and Florida (42) having the most locations, all still under family control.

The company has long collected daily transaction data from all stores. The CEO has tasked the cosmetics department manager with preparing a performance report analyzing key metrics — total sales, discount rate, profit margin, sales growth rate, and return rate — and to suggest one additional analysis. Using Microsoft SQL Server, the goal is to provide an insightful review of the Christmas season to help enhance the company's holiday sales strategy.

Project Summary: Dillard's Cosmetics Data Analysis

We analyzed Dillard's cosmetics data using SQL and Tableau. After preparing and cleaning the dataset, two new fields (Discount Rate and Profit Margin) were created.

Key analyses included:

  • Geographic Map: Compared state discount rates and profits across months.
  • Word Cloud: Highlighted top-selling brands in Florida and Louisiana with promotion suggestions.
  • Box Plots: Explored discount and sales distributions to check their relationship.
  • Clustering: Grouped products into "Sales Winners" and "Profit Winners" and identified outliers.
  • Sales Trends: Tracked monthly sales over years and forecasted next-year performance.
  • Return Analysis: Compared return rates with industry averages.
  • Story & Dashboard: Built an interactive Tableau dashboard and story to summarize insights.

Deliverable: A presentation with all visualizations and key insights.

Case Prerequisites

Students learned SQL basics and Tableau for data analysis. In week 11, they started the Dillard's case as a final individual project, submitted and presented in week 13. Pre- and post-tests showed clear improvement in SQL and Tableau skills. The case helps assess analytical abilities and can be used as a final or in-class project.

Applying the Case in Different Courses

Financial Accounting:

Instructors can simplify the case by:

  • Skipping data extraction and cleaning, providing the dataset in Excel.
  • Selecting only one or two analyses from the full list.
  • Assessing students' skill levels first — if they are not familiar with Tableau, they can be trained or use Excel instead.

Managerial Accounting:

KPI (Key Performance Indicator) analysis can be added using Tableau to evaluate whether the company meets its targets, with more focus on discussing results and providing managerial recommendations.

Graduate Courses:

Students perform more advanced tasks, such as:

  • Creating new variables or pivot columns using SQL instead of Tableau.
  • Interpreting statistical results from clustering analysis.
  • Merging Dillard's balance sheet data to perform financial ratio analysis.
  • Reviewing analyses to detect and explain outliers.

Common SQL Mistakes (Summary):

Students often:

  • Misname calculated fields (e.g., using spaces instead of underscores).
  • Use incorrect keys when joining tables.
  • Forget to filter data for the COSMETICS department.
  • Fail to exclude abnormal values or prevent division by zero.
  • Use double quotes instead of single quotes for dates.

Cluster Analysis:

  • Cluster 1: Profit Winners - are highly profitable but experience low sales. To attract new customers, our company could consider offering discounts or providing free samples of these products. Additionally, we can send free samples of these products to customers who purchase items from the Sales Winners cluster.
  • Cluster 2: Sales winners - are the most popular brands. To further capitalize on their popularity, our company can strategically place these products in prominent store locations or feature them prominently on the cosmetic products' webpage to attract a larger customer base. Given the mid-level profit margin, instead of offering discounts, our company could consider providing more generous gifts to incentivize purchases.
  • We can also conduct cluster analysis to analyze customers' behaviors. We can better understand the demands of different groups of customers to provide customized services.

h) Trend analysis

[Image d11: Trend analysis of sales growth rate - Replace this with d11.png]

Sales fluctuated significantly between January and August 2015, then increased rapidly after August until the end of the year. Although the sales growth rates decreased between January and August 2016, the decline was smoother compared to 2015.

Key Insights:

  • Since we only have partial data for October 2016 and missing data for November and December 2016, we can only compare the sales growth rate between January and October for 2015 and 2016. We observed that sales generally decreased from January to August in both 2015 and 2016.
  • The sales growth rates dropped sharply in March and August 2015, but not in 2016. These two declines in 2015 followed sales increases in February and June. The drops may have been caused by the cessation of promotional activities offered in February and June 2015. This issue appears to have been addressed in 2016.
  • Based on the sales growth rates for 2015 and 2016, we can predict that the sales growth rate for February 2017 may be close to 20%.
  • The line chart is especially good at discovering trends.
  • We can apply trend analysis to examine the trends in sales volume, profit growth, and other financial data.
  • We can use the word cloud to compare the sales growth rates of different brands. Additionally, we can use the geographic map to compare the sales growth rate of different states.
